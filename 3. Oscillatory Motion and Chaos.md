## 3.1 Simple Harmonic Motion

$$\begin{gather}
F=ma = m \frac{d^{2}\theta}{dt^{2}}=-mg\sin \theta\\
 m=-g\sin \theta
\end{gather}$$
displacement along the arc $s=l\theta, \to \theta=\frac{s}{l}$, 
assuming $\theta$ is small, $\sin \theta=\theta$ $$\frac{d^{2}\theta}{dt^{2}}=-\frac{g}{l}\theta$$

the general solution is $$\theta=\theta_{0}\sin(\Omega t+\phi)$$ where $\Omega=\sqrt{\frac{g}{l}}$  and $\theta_{0}$ and $\phi$ are constants that depends on the initial displacement and velocity the pendulum. 

The oscillations are sinusoidal with time and continue forever without decaying. The oscillations have angular frewuency, $\Omega$ but is independant of $m$ and the amplitude of the motion. 

Now consider a numerical approach to this probelm. (there is already the analystical exact solution but this is a precursor to more complicated oscillatory problems where exact solutions are not available )

The basic equation of motion is of the second order differenctial equation which we want to solve for $\theta$ as a function of $t$. It is convenient to rewrite this a stwo first order differential equations 
$$ \begin{eqnarray}
\frac{d\omega}{dt}&=&-\frac{g}{l}\theta\\ \frac{d\theta}{dt}&=&\omega
\end{eqnarray}$$
using ==[[Euler's Method]]. Convert  the above equations into difference equations using time steps $\Delta t$ so that time is discretized with $t=i\Delta t$ where $i$ is an integer== 

$$\begin{gather}
\frac{d\omega}{dt}=\frac{\omega _{i+1}-\omega_{i}}{\Delta t}=-\frac{g}{l}\theta_{i}\\\omega_{i+1}=\omega_{i}-\frac{g}{l}\theta_{i}\Delta t
\end{gather}$$
$$\begin{gather}
\frac{d\theta}{dt}=\frac{\theta_{i+1}-\theta_{i}}{\Delta t}=\omega\\ \theta_{i+1}=\theta_{i}+\omega\Delta t
\end{gather}$$
$\theta_i$ and $\omega_{i}$ is the numerically approximated angular displacement and velocity of the pendulum at time step $i$.

Since the model does not contain any source of energynor does it include any friction, the total energy of the pendulum should remain constant. Howver, our program does'nt seem to appreciate the fact!. The problem lies with the use of the Euler method. (even decreasing the time step $\Delta t$ does not fix the problem)

This is our first encounter with a numerical method that is inherently unstable. To see this instability, consider the total energy of the pendulum which is given by $$E=\frac{1}{2}ml^{2}\omega^{2}+mgl(1-\cos \theta)$$
where the first term is just the kinetic energy ($\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}$) where $v=\omega l$. The second term is the gravitational potential energy ($mgh$) where $h$ is the heightof the mass above the lowest point on its trajectory. In the limit of small $\theta$, the energy reduces to $$E=\frac{1}{2}ml^{2}\left( \omega^{2}+\frac{g}{l}\theta^{2} \right)$$
Substituting $\omega_{i+1}$ and $\theta_{i+1}$ from the Euler method into $\omega$ and $\theta$, the Euler result for energy at time $t_{i+1}$ is $$E_{i+1}=E_{i}+\frac{1}{2}mgl(\omega_{i}^{2}+\frac{g}{l}\theta_{i}^{2})(\Delta t)^{2}$$
since the second term on the right is always positive, the energy of the 'Euler solution' *always* increases with time, not matter how small we make $\Delta t$. 

But if the Euler method is unstable in this way, how did we manage to get away with using it for the problems in chapters 1 and 2? The answer to this is that a method can only be termed suitable or unsuitable in the context of a particular problem.  In the problems treated in chapters, 1 and 2, the oil limited also yielded in most cases, solutions that did not quite conserve energ.\n Y, however, in those situations, the areas were negligible on the skills relevant for those particular problems.\n In contrast, for problems involving oscillatory motion. We often want to consider the behaviour for many periods of oscillation. In order to be useful in such cases, a numerical method must conserve energy over the long Haul, the Euler method is not a good choice for these types of problems.

We are thus led to consider other methods for solving ordinary differential equations. This is such an important class of problems that we have devoted appendix A to a discussion of several different numerical approaches, including the Runge-Kutta and Verlet methods. While those work well in dealing with the oscillatory problems encountered in this chapter. It turns out that a simple modification of the Euler yields an algorithm that is also quite suitable, this modification yields, what is known as the Euler-Chroma method as Cromer was the first to carefully discuss the algorithm and show why it works to appreciate the Euler-Cromer method.We reconsider our pendulum program given below is a modified version of the calculate subroutine

What does it really mean to be chaotic? Your intuition probably tells you that chaotic behaviour is random and unpredictable, and the behaviour of our pendulum at high drive certainly has that appearance. However, if the behaviour is truly unpredictable, then how was our program able to calculate it? This conundrum can be put another way when we realise that the behaviour  Fear of our pendulum is described by the differential equation from the theory of such equations. We know that once the initial conditions (at $T= 0$, for example) are specified, the solution for data is then completely determined for all future times indeed, we took this for granted in constructing our program. But how can the behaviour be both deterministic and unpredictable at the same time?

We are thus faced with an apparent contradiction between analytic theory (the theory of differential equations) and numerical calculations (our program). Since our only evidence, so far, that the pendulum can be chaotic is from our numerical results, we might be tempted to suspect that we have made some sort of programming error or that we have somehow misinterpreted the meaning of the numerical results.  For example, we could imagine that if we waited long enough, a predictable pattern might emerge even at high drive, including at $F_D = 1.2$. Indeed, how can such behaviour ever be ruled out? Rather than pursue the question in this way, let us raise another possibility, namely that the behaviour is deterministic and unpredictable, at the same time. This may seem to be impossible but we will now show how to reconcile these two apparently contradictory notions.

Let's consider the stability of the solutions to the pendulum equation of motion. imagine two identical pendulum (same length, damping factors, $t_{0}$ and driving forces). The only difference is a slight difference in initial angles. Thus, calculate $\theta_{1}$ and $\theta_{2}$. The irregular variation of $\Delta \theta$ cannot be described by any simple function. However, if we repeat this calculation for a range of different inital values of $\theta_{1}$ (keeping $\Delta\theta(0)$ fixed) and average the results, we would find a much smoother behaviour such as the dashed line in figure from book. This line corresponds to the relation $\log(\Delta \theta)\sim \lambda t$. which imples $$\Delta \theta  \approx e^{\lambda t}$$
This functional form for $\Delta \theta$ is very common and the parameter $\lambda$ is known as Lyapunov exponent. 

For this pendulum, the numerical results show that $\lambda$ is positive at high drive, which means the two pendulums that start with nearly, but not exactly the same initial conditions will follow trajectories that diverge exponentially fast. Since we can never hope to know the initial conditions or any of the other pendulum parameters exactly, this means that the nehaviout at $F_{D}=1.2$ is for all practical purposes, unpredictable. The system is thus both deterministic and unpredictable. Put another way, a system can obey certain deterministic laws of physics, but still exhibit behaviour that is unpredictable due to an extreme sensitivity to initial conditions. *This* is what it means to be chaotic. 

The behaviour of figures $\Delta \theta$ can be described by Lyapunov exponent in both the chaotic and non chaotic regimes. In the former case, $\lambda>0$, while in the latter, $\lambda<0$. The transition to chaos thus occurs when $\lambda=0$.

## 3.4 Routes to Chaos : Period Doubling
https://youtu.be/1hCX5Gbeo0E
at low driving forces, the damped, nonlinear pendulum exhibit simple oscillatory motion, while at high drive it can be chaotic
How does the transition from simple to chaotic behaviour take place? The pendulum exhibits transitions to chaotic behaviour at several values of the driving force. However, this transition is not the clearest one to study numerically, so we will instead consider the behaviour at somewhat higher driving forces. 

![[Drawing 2025-09-25 17.34.32.excalidraw|1000]]

At high values of the drive, the pendulum often swings all the way around; This can be seen from the vertical steps in $\theta$ as the program resets (to keep the angle $[\pi,\pi]$. ) These steps notwithstanding , the behaviour in figure above is a periodic, repeating function of $t$ in all three cases (after the initial transients have damped away). 

$\Omega_{D}=\frac{2}{3}, T=3\pi$ and the period is precisely the period found at $F_{D}=1.35$. In this case, the pendulum moves with the same frequency as the driving force. 

at $F_{D}=1.44$, while it is periodic motion, the period is *twice* the drive period. When a nonlinear is excited or driven by a single frequency stimulus, the response is, in general not limited to the driving frequency. If $\Omega_{D}$ is the driving frequency, the nonlinear response will usually contain components of $2\Omega_{D}, 3\Omega_{D}$, etc., at all harmonics. This process is known as mixing and is manifest by the generation of responses at integer multiples of the driving frequency. Such a nonlinear response is standard and is well understood, and its key property is that it contains frequencies that are equal to or greater than the drive frequency. Hence, the period of these harmonics will be smaller than the drive period. In contrast the pendulum is now exhibiting a response at $\frac{\Omega_{D}}{2}$ (a lower frequency), a *subharmonic* which is unlike any standard mixing effect. 

results for $F_{D}=1.465$ shows that they exhibit a period that is four times the driving period. If were to increase the driving force further, the period would double again. 

But if the period keeps on doubling, what about the transition to chaos? A bifurcation diagram is a nice way to appreciate this transition. In figure below, is shown a bifurcation diagram for $\theta$ as a function of drive amplitude. 

![[Pasted image 20250928091540.png]]
the way it is constructed: 
1. For each vlaue $F_{D}$, $\theta$ is calculated as a function of time.
2. After waiting 399 driving periods (transient), plot $\theta$ at times that were in phase with the driving force (like the Poincare section) as a function of $F_{D}$. 
3. THe process is repeated for the range of $F_{D}$ in figure. 

at $F_{D}=1.35$, the motion has the same period as the drive, so if we observe $\theta$ at a particular time in the drive cycle we will always find the same value. The bifurcation diagram thus consists of a single point; there is just one value of $\theta$ for this value of $F_{D}$, although that point will be plotted many times. This is a period-1 behaviour.

Period-2, the values of $\theta$ that are plotted will alternate between two values. This is just the alternation seen in $F_{D}=1.44$ and it leads to two points on the bifurcation diagram. 

Motion that is period $n$ will yield $n$ points on the bifurcation diagram for that value of $F_{D}$. This persists up to the transition to period-4 behaviour at $F_{D} \approx 1.459$. This process continues although the resolution of the diagram makes it difficult to follow thw period up to period 8. This period-doubling cascade ultimately ends in a transition to chaotic behaviour. 

##### universality 
Many systems have been found to exhibit chaotic behaviour, but there appear to be only a few ways in which the transition from simple to chaotic behaviour can occur. The period doubling scenario is one of them. 

the spacing between period doubling transitions become rapidly smaller as the order of the transition increases. Let $F_{n}$ be the value of the driving force at which the transition to period $2^n$ behaviour takes place. The shrinkage of the size of the periodic windows can be described by a parameter $\delta_{n}$ where $$\delta_{n}=\frac{F_{n}-F_{n-1}}{F_{n+1}-F_{n}}$$
The observation that the windows become smaller as $n$ increases means that $\delta_{n}>1$. It has been found that as $n$ becomes large, $\delta_{n}$ approaches a constant known as the Feigenbaum $\delta$. That is, the rate of the shrinkage approaches a constant in the limit $n \rightarrow \infty$. Moreover, it turns out that all systems that exhibit period doubling route to chaos appear to possess the same value of $\delta \approx 4.669$. 
This is one of the universal parameters and features associated with the transition to chaos.

Consider the behaviour of the logistic map for small values of $\mu$ ($\mu=2.0$). The system rapidly approaches a particular of $x$ that is called a *fixed point*. That is, after many iterations the map repeats the same value of $x$, over and over. If we define the logistic function $$f(x)=\mu x(1-x)\tag{1}$$
then the fixed points of $x^*$ of the map function satisfy the relation $$x^*=f(x^*)\tag{2}$$(1) and (2) can be combined to find the exact fixed point values(s) $x^*$ as a function of $\mu$. However ,it is very costructive to consider the fixed point behaviour graphically as shown below

Here, the map function $f(x)$ with $\mu=1.5$ is plotted, along with a slope of unity ($y=x$). That is both sides of (2) is plotted. The fixed points are then the values of $x$ at which these two curves intersect. If the value of $x$ somehow gets to a fixed point value (ie. $x_{n}=x^*$), then $x_{n+1}$ will repeat the value and then $x_{n+2}$ will repeat... 

The dotted line in figure above is the trajectory of the system starting from an initial value $x_{0}=0.5$. The path taken: Starting from the point $[(x_{0},0)]$ on the horizontal axis, the system first moves to the point $[x_{0},f(x_{0})]$. It then contniues on, passing through the points $[x_{1}, f(x_{1})]$, $[x_{2},f(x_{2})]$ etc, eventually approaching the fixed point at $[x^*, f(x^*)]$.

## 3.6 The Lorenz Model 

>[!cite] 
Lorenz was studying the Navier Stokes equations (the basic equations of fluid dynamics). These are a complicated set of differential equations that describe the velocity, temperature, density, etc., as functions of position and time, and they are very difficult to solve analytically in cases of practical interest. The specific situation Lorenz considered was the Rayleigh-Benard problem, which concerns a fluid in a container whose top and bottom surfaces are held at different temperatures.  - As the difference between these two temperature increased, the fluid can undergo transitions from a stationary state (no fluid motion) to steady flow (nonzero flow velocities that are constant in time, also referred to as convection) to chaotic flow. 

Lorenz consider a greatly simplified version of the Navier-Stokes equations as applied to this particular problem. Indeed, he grossly oversimplified the problem as he reduced it to only three equations
$$\begin{lgathered}
\frac{dx}{dt}= \sigma(y-x) \\
\frac{dy}{dt}=-xz+rz-y \\
\frac{dz}{dt} = xy-bz 
\end{lgathered}$$
These are known as the Lorenz equations (or the Lorenz model). The Navier-Stokes from which the equations above is derived from involve the state of the fluid as a function of position and time. 

>[!abstract] numerical aspects of the problem 
>From the pendulum equation os motion, the Euler method does not conserve energy for oscillatory problems. 
>The Lorenz equations exhibit oscillatory solution for certain parameter values
>However, Euler-Cromer is designed for second order differential equations, it is not directly applicable to the Lorenz model 
>- as it turns out, the Euler algorithm can actually be used to treat the Lorenz problem. For the following reasons: 
>    - Several of the terms in the Lorenz equations play the same role as the damping term in the pendulum equations of motions
>    - While other terms are analogous to the driving force. 
> - If the time steo in the Euler algorithm is sufficiently small, the energy lost or added through the error terms associated with the Euler method can be made much smaller than the energy lost to the effective damping, or added by the driving force 

The Lorenz problem has three parameters, $\delta,b,r$ and the behaviour one finds depends on their values. as custom : use $\delta=10, b=\frac{8}{3}$. 
- The parameter $r$ is a measure of the temperature difference between the top and bottom sufaces of the fluid. 
    - For small $r$, the effective force on the fluid is small in the sense that there is very little heat carried by the fluid. 
    - As $r$ increases this force increases, 
    - so $r$ plays a role analogous to the drive amplitude, $F_{D}$

# 3.7 The Billiard Problem 
consider the problem of a call moving without friction on a horizontal table. Imagine that there are walls at the edges of the table that reflect the ball perfectly and that there is no frictional force between the ball and the table. Think of this as a billiard ball that moves without friction on a perfect billiard table. The ball is given some initial velocitu, and the problem is to calculate and understanf the resulting trajectory. This is known as the stadium billiard problem. 

Exccept for the collisions with the walls, the motion of the billiard is quite simple. Between collisions the velocity is constant so we have, $$\begin{eqnarray}
\frac{dx}{dt}=v_{x}\\\frac{dy}{dt}=v_{y} \tag{1}
\end{eqnarray}$$
where $v_{x}$ and $v_{y}$ change only through collisions with the wall. Euler's algorithm can be used. Velocity is constant (except in collisions), so the Euler solution gives an exact description of the motion across the table. 

The most difficult part of the calculations is the treatment of the collisions. Assuming perfect elasticity, the reflections will be mirrorlike,  (the angle of incidence = angle of reflection).
